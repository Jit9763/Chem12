Azeotropes & Colligative Properties
(Chapter 1: Solutions)

❖ Azeotropic Mixtures –

"Liquid-liquid mixtures which have the same composition in the liquid and vapour phase and boil at a constant temperature are called Azeotropes."

Note: The components of an azeotropic mixture cannot be separated by Fractional Distillation.

1. Minimum Boiling Azeotrope –

These are mixtures that show a large positive deviation from Raoult's law at a specific composition. Their boiling point is lower than that of either component.
Example: Water + Ethanol (95% Ethanol by volume).

2. Maximum Boiling Azeotrope –

These are mixtures that show a large negative deviation from Raoult's law at a specific composition. Their boiling point is higher than that of either component.
Example: Nitric acid (68%) + Water (32%) [Boiling Point = 393.5 K].

❖ Colligative Properties of Solutions

"The properties of solutions which depend only on the number of solute particles (solute concentration) and not on their chemical identity or nature are called Colligative Properties."

1. Relative Lowering of Vapour Pressure
2. Elevation of Boiling Point (ΔTb)
3. Depression of Freezing Point (ΔTf)
4. Osmotic Pressure (π)

❖ 1. Relative Lowering of Vapour Pressure –

  • Moles of Solvent = n1
  • Moles of Solute      = n2
  • For dilute solutions: n2 ≪ n1
  • Solute Mass & Molar Mass: = W2 & M2
  • Solvent Mass & Molar Mass: = W1 & M1
  • X2 = n2 / n1+n2

"When a non-volatile/non-electrolyte solute (solid) is added to a pure solvent, the relative lowering of the vapour pressure of the solution is equal to the mole fraction of the solute."

From Raoult's Law:   P = P1° . X1

Lowering of Vapour Pressure:   ΔP1 = P1° - P1

ΔP1 = P1° - P1° . X1

ΔP1 = P1° (1 - X1)      [Since X2 = 1 - X1]

ΔP1 = P1° X2

ΔP1P1° = X2      (Where ΔP1 = P1° - P1)

P1° - P1P1° = X2      (Relative Lowering of Vapour Pressure term)

P1° - P1P1° = n2n1 + n2

P1° - P1P1° = n2n1      (For dilute solutions n2 ≪ n1)

P1° - P1P1° = W2 / M2W1 / M1   ⇒   P1° - P1P1° = W2 . M1M2 . W1

Molar Mass of Non-volatile Solute (MB):
MB = WB × MAWA   .   (PA°PA° - PA)

❖ 2. Elevation of Boiling Point (क्वथनांक उन्नयन - ΔTb) –

When a non-volatile solute (अवाष्पशील विलेय) is added to a pure solvent, its vapour pressure (वाष्पदाब) decreases. This means the mixture needs more heat (ताप) to boil. The boiling point (क्वथनांक) of the solution becomes higher than the pure solvent. This increase is called Elevation in Boiling Point.

(Boiling Point / क्वथनांक: The temperature at which Vapour Pressure equals Atmospheric Pressure (वायुमण्डलीय दाब).)
  • ✓ B.P. of Solution (विलयन) = Tb
  • ✓ B.P. of Pure solvent (विलायक) = Tb°
  • ✓ Tb > Tb°
  • ✓ Mass & Molar Mass of Solute = W2 & M2
  • ✓ Mass & Molar Mass of Solvent = W1 & M1

Elevation of Boiling Point (ΔTb) is directly proportional to the molal concentration (m) of the solute.

Step 1: Difference in Boiling Point (क्वथनांक में अंतर)

ΔTb = Tb - Tb°

Step 2: According to Raoult's Law (राऊल्ट के नियम के अनुसार)

ΔTb ∝ m (Molality)

ΔTb = Kb . m

Step 3: Putting value of Molality (मोललता का मान रखने पर)

m = W2 × 1000M2 × W1

ΔTb = Kb . W2 × 1000M2 × W1

Final: Molar Mass of Solute (विलेय का आणविक द्रव्यमान)
M2 = Kb × W2 × 1000ΔTb × W1

Molal Elevation Constant (Kb):

If 1 mole of solute is dissolved in 1Kg solvent, then ΔTb = Kb

❖ Depression of Freezing Point (ΔTf)

The freezing point of a solvent decreases when a non-volatile solute is added. This means the solution freezes at a lower temperature than the pure solvent. This phenomenon is called the Depression in Freezing Point.

ΔTf = Tf° (Pure Solvent) – Tf (Solution)
ΔTf ∝ m (Molality)
ΔTf = Kf . m

Kf = Molal Depression Constant or Cryoscopic Constant.
