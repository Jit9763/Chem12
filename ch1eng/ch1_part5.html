❖ Vapour Pressure –

The pressure exerted by the vapour phase particles on the liquid surface at equilibrium between evaporation and condensation in a closed vessel at constant temperature is called Vapour Pressure or Saturated Vapour Pressure.

❖ Raoult’s Law – Quantitative Relationship between Partial Pressure and Mole Fraction

At constant temperature, the partial vapour pressure of each component of a solution is directly proportional to its mole fraction present in the solution.

Partial Pressure of component (P) = Mole Fraction (x) X Vapour Pressure of pure component (P°)
P₁ ∝ x₁      ;      P₁ = P₁° . x₁ ......... (i)
P₂ ∝ x₂      ;      P₂ = P₂° . x₂ ......... (ii)

1. Raoult’s Law for Volatile Solute Solutions –

Partial Pressure (P) = Mole Fraction (x) X Pure Component Vapour Pressure (P°)

If a binary solution contains two volatile components, solvent 1 and solute 2:

P₁ ∝ x₁
P₁ = P₁° . x₁ ...............(i)

P₂ ∝ x₂
P₂ = P₂° . x₂ ...............(ii)
P₁ = Partial pressure of solvent
P₂ = Partial pressure of solute
x₁ = Mole fraction of solvent
x₂ = Mole fraction of solute
P₁° = Vapour pressure of pure solvent
P₂° = Vapour pressure of pure solute

From Dalton’s Law of Partial Pressures: P = P₁ + P₂

P = P₁° . x₁ + P₂° . x₂ ............(iii)
if x₁ + x₂ = 1 ; x₁ = 1 - x₂
P = P₁°(1 - x₂) + P₂° . x₂
P = P₁° + x₂(P₂° - P₁°)
Conclusion:
1. Total vapour pressure of the solution can be related to the mole fraction of any one component.
2. Total vapour pressure varies linearly with the mole fraction of the solute.
# Raoult’s Law as a Special Case of Henry’s Law: When a gas is dissolved in a liquid, the gaseous component, being volatile, exists in the gaseous form. Thus, its partial pressure is proportional to its mole fraction. Only the proportionality constant differs. In this way, Raoult's Law becomes a special case of Henry's Law where KH becomes equal to P₁°.

P₁ = P₁° . x₁      ;      P = KH . x      ;      KH = P₁°

2. Raoult’s Law for Solutions containing Non-volatile Solutes –

"The relative lowering of vapour pressure of a solution containing a non-volatile solute is equal to the mole fraction of the solute."

Lowering of VP = VP of Pure Solvent (P°) - VP of Solution (P)
P₁° – P = Lowering of Vapour Pressure

P₁° – P P₁°
= Relative Lowering of Vapour Pressure

❖ Ideal Solution

Ideal Solution

➤ The solution which obeys Raoult's law over the entire range of concentration and temperature.

➤ Characteristics of Ideal Solutions –

  1. They obey Raoult's law.
    [P₁ = P₁° . x₁   ;   P₂ = P₂° . x₂]
  2. Change in volume on mixing is zero (ΔmixV = 0)
  3. Change in enthalpy on mixing is zero (ΔmixH = 0)
  4. Intermolecular attraction: A-A & B-B is equal to A-B attraction.
  5. Solute and solvent with similar properties form ideal solutions, but a perfect ideal solution is not possible.

➤ Examples –

[n-hexane + n-heptane], [ethyl chloride + ethyl bromide], [benzene + toluene], [Cl-benzene + Br-benzene], [methanol + ethanol]

❖ Non-ideal Solution

➤ Solutions that do not obey Raoult's law at all ranges of temperature and concentration.

➤ Characteristics –

  • 1. V.P. Deviation:
    [P₁ ≠ P₁° . x₁   ;   P₂ ≠ P₂° . x₂]
  • 2. Volume Change: ΔmixV ≠ 0
  • 3. Enthalpy Change: ΔmixH ≠ 0

❖ Positive Deviation

When the vapour pressure of a solution is higher than that predicted by Raoult's law, it is called Positive Deviation.

➤ Reason:

Intermolecular Attraction:
A-A & B-B > A-B
(Attraction between A and B is weaker, making it easier for molecules to escape)

➤ Properties:

• P₁ > P₁° . x₁   ;   P₂ > P₂° . x₂
• ΔmixV = +ive (Expansion)
• ΔmixH = +ive (Endothermic)

➤ Examples:

1. Acetone + Ethanol
2. Acetone + CS₂
3. Ethanol + Water
4. Methanol + CCl₄

❖ Negative Deviation

When the vapour pressure of a solution is lower than that predicted by Raoult's law, it is called Negative Deviation.

➤ Reason:

Intermolecular Attraction:
A-A & B-B < A-B
(Attraction between A and B is stronger, making it harder for molecules to escape)

➤ Properties:

• P₁ < P₁° . x₁   ;   P₂ < P₂° . x₂
• ΔmixV = -ive (Contraction)
• ΔmixH = -ive (Exothermic)

➤ Examples:

1. Acetone + Chloroform (H-bond)
2. Phenol + Aniline
3. Methanol + Acetic Acid
4. Chloroform + Benzene
