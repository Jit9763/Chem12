Solubility & Henry's Law
(Chapter 1)

❖ Solubility –

The maximum amount of solute that can be dissolved or present in a specific amount of solvent at a concrete temperature.
(a) Solubility of Solida in Liquids
(b) Solubility of Gases in Liquids

(a) Solubility of Solids in Liquids –

The maximum amount of a solid solute in grams present in 100 grams of a liquid solvent at a specific temperature is called its solubility. This is a saturated solution state.

  • The maximum amount of solute dissolved in a fixed quantity of solvent (i.e., the concentration of solute in saturated solutions) is its solubility.
  • The process of a solid solute dissolving in a liquid solvent is called dissolution.
  • The separation of solute particles from a solution is called crystallization.
  • When the rate of dissolution and crystallization becomes equal, dynamic equilibrium is established in the solution.
  • When no more solute can be dissolved in a solution at a given temperature and pressure, it is called a saturated solution.
  • When more solute can be dissolved in a solution at a given temperature and pressure, it is called an unsaturated solution.

Factors Affecting Solubility of Solids in Liquids –

1. Nature of Solute and Solvent: "Like dissolves Like"
Example: Salt and sugar are highly soluble in water but do not dissolve in benzene.
Naphthalene and anthracene are highly soluble in benzene but do not dissolve in water.

Ionic solids dissolve in polar solvents (eg- NaCl, KCl, KNO₃ + WATER): Reason – Ion-dipole interactions.
Covalent or non-polar solids dissolve in non-polar solvents (eg - I₂, S₈, + CCl₄ / CS₂).

Extra Key Note:

  • # Hydration Energy > Lattice Energy = High solubility of ionic solids in water.
  • # Hydration Energy < Lattice Energy = Low solubility of ionic solids in water.
  • # Lattice Energy – Energy required to separate one mole of an ionic solid into its gaseous ions.
  • # Hydration Energy – Energy released during the hydration of ions present in one mole of an ionic solid.

2. Temperature: Equilibrium exists between the undissolved solid and the solution in a saturated solution.
Hence: Undissolved Solid + Solvent ⇌ Solution [ΔH = ± ve]

  • ➢ [ΔH = + ve] or [ΔH>0] → Endothermic process, Le Chatelier's Principle: Temp↑ Solubility↑ [eg. NH₄Cl, KCl, KI]
  • ➢ [ΔH = - ve] or [ΔH<0] → Exothermic process, Le Chatelier's Principle: Temp↑ Solubility↓ [eg. NaOH, Li₂SO₄]

3. Effect of Pressure: Solubility of solids in liquids is unaffected by pressure because solids and liquids are generally incompressible.

(b) Solubility of Gases in Liquids: Absorption of Gases by Liquids

"Absorption Coefficient": The volume (ml) of gas dissolved in 1 ml volume of water is called the absorption coefficient.

Factors Affecting Solubility of Gases –

  1. Nature of Gas (Critical Temp↑ Liquefaction↑ Solubility↑, hence Polarity and Reactivity↑)
  2. Order of absorption coefficient of gases in 1cm³ water: NH₃ > HCl > SO₂ > H₂S > CO₂ > C₂H₂ > O₂ > N₂
  3. Nature of Solvent (Like dissolves like)
  4. Temp α 1/Solubility     Hence Temp↑ Kinetic Energy of particles↑ Solubility↓     (Dissolution is exothermic)
  5. Pressure α Solubility     Hence Pressure↑ Solubility↑

Henry's Law (Effect of Pressure on Gas Solubility) –

At constant temperature, the solubility of a gas in a liquid is directly proportional to the partial pressure of the gas present above the surface of the liquid or solution.
The partial pressure of the gas in the vapour phase (p) is proportional to the mole fraction (x) of the gas in the solution.

p α x ; p = KH x (KH = Henry's Constant)

Note: The value of Henry's constant depends on the nature of the gas. Hence      KH α 1/Solubility

1.13 Applications of Henry's Law

  1. Aquatic Life and Cold Water: As temperature increases, the value of KH increases and the solubility of O₂ in water decreases. Therefore, aquatic animals (fish, etc.) feel more comfortable in cold water than in warm water because more oxygen is dissolved in cold water.
  2. Soft Drinks: To increase the solubility of CO₂ in soft drinks or soda water, the bottle is sealed under high pressure.
  3. Scuba Divers and Bends: Since pressure increases underwater, the solubility of atmospheric gases (N₂) in the diver's blood increases. When the diver comes to the surface, the pressure decreases and the dissolved N₂ comes out as bubbles, blocking capillaries. This extremely painful and dangerous condition is called Bends.
    * To avoid this, scuba tanks are filled with air diluted with Helium.
  4. Altitude and Anoxia: At high altitudes, atmospheric pressure is low, so the concentration (solubility) of O₂ in blood decreases. This causes climbers to feel weak and reduces their ability to think clearly, a condition known as Anoxia.

Limitations of Henry's Law:

  • Pressure should not be extremely high and temperature should not be extremely low.
  • The gas should not undergo any chemical change (reaction), association or dissociation with the solvent.
  • The solution should be dilute.
