🟢 Short Answer Questions - 10
Q1
Between Molarity and Molality, which is affected by temperature and why?
Ans: Molarity is affected by temperature because it depends on the Volume of the solution, which changes with temperature. Molality depends solely on the mass of the solvent, hence it remains completely independent of temperature changes.
Q2
Write any two limitations of Henry's Law.
Ans: (1) The pressure of the gas should not be extremely high and the temperature should not be extremely low. (2) The gas should not undergo any chemical reaction or dissociation/association in the solvent (e.g., NH₃ in water forms NH₄OH, so Henry's law fails here).
Q3
What are Colligative Properties?
Ans: All physical properties of a solution that depend exclusively on the total number of solute particles (ions or molecules) present in the solution, and not on the chemical nature of the solute, are termed Colligative Properties.
Q4
What is a Semipermeable Membrane (SPM)?
Ans: It is a special selective membrane that allows only the small molecules of the solvent to pass through its pores, effectively preventing the passage of larger solute molecules. Example: Copper ferrocyanide (Cu₂[Fe(CN)₆]) artificial membrane.
Q5
Define Van't Hoff factor (i).
Ans: The ratio of the experimentally observed value of a colligative property to the theoretically calculated value of that property is called the Van't Hoff factor. It is denoted by 'i'.

i = Observed Colligative PropertyTheoretical Colligative Property   or   i = Normal Molar MassAbnormal Molar Mass
Q6
What are Ideal Solutions?
Ans: Solutions that strictly obey Raoult's Law across all ranges of temperature and concentration are termed Ideal Solutions. Upon the formation of an ideal solution, the enthalpy of mixing (∆H_mix) is zero and the volume of mixing (∆V_mix) is also zero.
Q7
Write Raoult's Law.
Ans: According to Raoult's law, at a given constant temperature, for a solution containing volatile liquids, the partial vapour pressure of each liquid component in the solution is directly proportional to its mole fraction (P₁ = P₁° × X₁).
Q8
Write the formula for Osmotic Pressure.
Ans: According to the Van't Hoff equation, the formula for Osmotic Pressure is: π = CRT. (Where π = Osmotic Pressure, C = Molar concentration of solution, R = Universal gas/solution constant, T = Absolute Temperature in Kelvin). If dissociation/association occurs, the formula is π = iCRT.
Q9
What is Reverse Osmosis (RO)?
Ans: If an external pressure greater than the osmotic pressure (π) is applied to the solution side (higher concentration), the solvent molecules start flowing in the reverse direction, i.e., from the solution into the pure solvent area. This phenomenon is called Reverse Osmosis. Its primary application is the desalination of seawater.
Q10
What are Azeotropic mixtures?
Ans: A liquid mixture composed of two or more distinct volatile liquids which has exactly the same composition in both liquid phase and vapour phase, and which boils at a constant, identical temperature without undergoing any fractional change is called an Azeotropic mixture.
🔵 Long Answer Questions - 5
Q1
Explain Raoult's Law and differentiate between Ideal and Non-ideal solutions based on it.
Ans:

Raoult's Law:
French chemist Raoult formulated a law in 1886 for solutions of volatile liquids. According to this law — 'At a given temperature, for a solution of volatile liquids, the partial vapour pressure of each component in the solution is directly proportional to its mole fraction.'
If a solution contains two volatile components A and B, according to Raoult's law:
P_A ∝ X_A ⟹ P_A = P_A° × X_A
P_B ∝ X_B ⟹ P_B = P_B° × X_B
Where P_A° and P_B° are vapour pressures in the pure state. By Dalton's law of partial pressures, the total vapour pressure is P_total = P_A + P_B = P_A°X_A + P_B°X_B.

Difference between Ideal and Non-Ideal Solutions:

Property Ideal Solution Non-Ideal Solution
1. Raoult's Law Strictly obeys Raoult's law under all ranges of temperature and concentration (P_A = P_A°X_A). Does NOT obey Raoult's law (P_A ≠ P_A°X_A).
2. Enthalpy Change (∆H) Enthalpy of mixing is zero (∆H_mix = 0). No heat is absorbed or evolved. Enthalpy of mixing is not zero (∆H_mix ≠ 0). Heat is either absorbed or evolved.
3. Volume Change (∆V) Volume of mixing is zero (∆V_mix = 0). Volume of mixing is not zero (∆V_mix ≠ 0). Volume expands or contracts.
4. Intermolecular forces Attractive forces between A-A & B-B are perfectly identical to A-B forces. Attractive forces between A-A & B-B are stronger/weaker than A-B forces.
5. Examples Benzene + Toluene, n-Hexane + n-Heptane Ethanol + Acetone (Positive dev.), Chloroform + Acetone (Negative dev.)
Q2
What do you understand by Elevation in Boiling Point? Derive the formula to calculate molar mass of solute.
Ans:

Elevation in Boiling Point:
The boiling point of a liquid is the temperature at which its vapour pressure becomes precisely equal to the external atmospheric pressure (usually 1 atm). When a non-volatile solute is added to a pure volatile solvent, the vapour pressure of the resultant solution always decreases compared to the pure solvent. Because of this lowered vapour pressure, the solution must be heated to a significantly higher temperature so its vapour pressure can reach 1 atm. Therefore, the boiling point of the solution is greater than that of the pure solvent. This specific increase in boiling point is called the Elevation in Boiling Point (∆T_b).
∆T_b = T_b (Boiling point of solution) – T_b° (Boiling point of pure solvent)

Derivation of Molar Mass Formula:
Experimentally, it has been conclusively proven that for dilute solutions, the elevation in boiling point (∆T_b) is directly proportional to the molal concentration (molality, m) of the solute in the solution.
∆T_b ∝ m
∆T_b = K_b × m    ......(Equation 1)
(Where K_b is the Ebullioscopic constant or molal elevation constant, and m is molality, which is moles of solute dissolved per 1000 g of solvent).

Molality (m) = Mass of solute (W_B)Molar Mass of solute (M_B) × 1000Mass of solvent (W_A)

Substituting the value of molality (m) in Equation 1, we get:
∆T_b = K_b ×
W_B × 1000
M_B × W_A


Rearranging the formula to find the Molar Mass of the solute (M_B):
M_B =
1000 × K_b × W_B
∆T_b × W_A
Q3
What is Osmotic Pressure? Describe the Berkeley-Hartley method.
Ans:

Osmotic Pressure:
When a solution is completely separated from its pure solvent by a Semipermeable Membrane (SPM), the solvent molecules intrinsically begin flowing through the membrane into the solution (Osmosis). The minimum excess external pressure that must be applied precisely to the solution side in order to just stop this flow of solvent (stop osmosis) is defined as the Osmotic Pressure (π) of the solution.

Berkeley-Hartley Method:
This is the most superior and modern laboratory method to precisely and safely measure osmotic pressure.
Principle: This method utilizes a robust artificial semipermeable membrane made of Copper Ferrocyanide (Cu₂[Fe(CN)₆]).
Apparatus & Working:
1. The apparatus consists of a central porous tube containing the artificial copper ferrocyanide membrane deposited within its pores. This inner tube is filled completely with pure water (solvent).
2. One end of this porous tube is fitted with a water reservoir, and the other end is fitted with a fine capillary tube to observe the water level.
3. This inner tube is enclosed within an outer strong Gun-metal jacket/cylinder containing the solution whose osmotic pressure is to be determined. A pressure gauge and a mechanical pressure piston are connected to this outer jacket.
4. Initially, due to natural osmosis, water molecules from the inner tube flow outwards into the surrounding solution. As a result, the water level visibly drops in the capillary indicator tube.
5. Slowly, external pressure is strategically applied onto the solution via the piston. Pressure is increased just enough so that the falling water level in the capillary is forced back to its exact original initial mark, and becomes stationary.
6. This stationary level indicates that the external pressure applied has exactly counterbalanced and stopped the osmosis process. This pressure is read directly from the pressure gauge, giving the exact Osmotic Pressure of the solution.
Q4
What is Depression in Freezing Point? How will you determine the molar mass of a non-volatile solute based on it?
Ans:

Depression in Freezing Point:
The freezing point of a substance is defined strictly as the temperature at which its liquid phase and solid phase exist together in dynamic equilibrium, meaning both phases have precisely the exact same vapour pressure. When a non-volatile solute is dissolved into a pure solvent, the vapour pressure of the resulting solution abruptly decreases. Due to this significant lowering of vapour pressure, the solution will now freeze at a noticeably lower temperature compared to the pure solvent. This distinct decrease or drop in the freezing temperature is called the Depression in Freezing Point (∆T_f).
∆T_f = T_f° (Freezing Point of pure solvent) – T_f (Freezing point of solution)

Determination of Molar Mass:
Experimentally and consistently with Raoult's Law, for very dilute solutions, this depression in freezing point is directly directly proportional to the molal concentration (molality, m) of the solute.
∆T_f ∝ m
∆T_f = K_f × m    (Where K_f is the Cryoscopic constant or molal depression constant).

We know that Molality (m) = W_B × 1000M_B × W_A
Substituting this value into the equation gives:
∆T_f = K_f × W_B × 1000M_B × W_A

Rearranging for Molar mass of the non-volatile solute (M_B):
M_B =
1000 × K_f × W_B
∆T_f × W_A
Q5
What is Abnormal Molar Mass? How is the degree of dissociation and association calculated using the Van't Hoff factor?
Ans:

Abnormal Molar Mass:
When a solute is dissolved into a solvent, and it either splits violently into multiple ions (Dissociation) or its molecules hydrogen-bond together to form larger dimers/polymers (Association), the total aggregate number of particles in the solution changes unpredictably. Since experimental Molar Mass is mathematically inversely proportional inversely to the number of particles (colligative properties), the experimentally observed molar mass of the solute turns out to be entirely different (either falsely higher or lower) from its true, normal expected molar mass. This drastically altered or incorrect molar mass is termed identically as 'Abnormal Molar Mass'.

Van't Hoff Factor (i):
Dutch scientist Jacobus Henricus van 't Hoff elegantly introduced the factor 'i' to explicitly account for this extreme deviation.
i = Total observed moles of particles after dissociation/associationNormal initial moles of particles before dissociation/association

1. Calculating Degree of Dissociation (α):
If one molecule breaks apart entirely to yield 'n' ions (e.g., NaCl yields 2 ions, thus n=2):
Formula: α = i - 1n - 1
If the solute is 100% completely dissociated (strong electrolyte, α = 1), then fundamentally i = n.

2. Calculating Degree of Association (α):
If 'n' discrete molecules bond together to form 1 single associated molecule (e.g., 2 Acetic acid molecules form 1 dimer, thus n=2):
Formula: α = 1 - i1 - 1n   or   α = i - 11n - 1
Because the total number of particles dramatically decreases, the value of 'i' for association is universally less than 1 (i < 1).

Volume of Solution (V) = 200 ml

Therefore, the molarity of the solution is 0.5 M (mol/L).

Molar Mass of Urea (M_B) = 60 g/mol
Temperature (T) = 298 K
Universal Gas Constant (R) = 0.0821 L atm K⁻¹ mol⁻¹

Osmotic Pressure (π) from Van't Hoff Equation:
π = CRT or π = nVRT or π = W_BM_B × V × R × T

π = 560 × 0.1 × 0.0821 × 298

π = 56 × 24.4658

π = 0.8333 × 24.4658 = 20.38 atm

Thus, the osmotic pressure is firmly 20.38 atm.

Q5
The Van't Hoff factor for 1m NaCl solution is 1.9. Calculate its degree of dissociation (α).
Solution:
Given Data: i = 1.9
NaCl is a notoriously strong electrolyte that rapidly completely dissociates into exactly two distinct ions in water:
NaCl ⟶ Na⁺ + Cl⁻
Thus, here 'n' (total number of discrete ions yielded) = 2

Formula for the Degree of Dissociation (α):
α = i - 1n - 1

Substituting the known values:
α = 1.9 - 12 - 1

α = 0.91 = 0.9

Converting to Percentage Dissociation:
α% = 0.9 × 100% = 90%

Therefore, the NaCl strongly dissociates by exactly 90% in this specific solution.

B1
Give an example of a non-ideal solution showing positive deviation. (RBSE 2019)
Ans: A solution of Ethanol and Acetone perfectly exemplifies this. In this mixture, the new A-B intermolecular attractive forces become significantly weaker than the pure A-A and B-B forces, causing the molecules to escape easily and vapour pressure to rise above Raoult's prediction.
B2
What are isotonic solutions? (RBSE 2020)
Ans: Isotonic solutions are any two distinct solutions having exactly the same identical osmotic pressure (π₁ = π₂) at identically the same given temperature. Crucially, when these solutions are physically separated exclusively by a semipermeable membrane, absolutely no net osmosis flow occurs between them.
B3
Why is Helium added to scuba diver's cylinders? (RBSE 2022)
Ans: Deep underwater at extreme high pressure, nitrogen gas from regular air viciously dissolves into the diver's blood in massively massive quantities. When the diver ascends swiftly back to the surface, the atmospheric pressure violently plummets, causing the dissolved nitrogen to rapidly bubble out inside the blood capillaries. This severely blocks blood flow and triggers an intensely painful, agonizing, and potentially fatal medical condition famously known as the 'Bends'. Because Helium maintains exceedingly low solubility in blood even under crushing underwater pressure, it is meticulously mixed into scuba cylinders to flawlessly eliminate this life-threatening risk.
