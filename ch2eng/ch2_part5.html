Conductance of Electrolytic Solutions

Part 5: Conductivity & Classification of Matter

⚡ 1. Electrical Resistance (R)

The obstruction offered by any conductor or solution to the flow of electric current is called Resistance.

Concept Analogy
Think of it like water flowing through a pipe:
• Long pipe ($l$) = More friction, making it harder for water to flow (Higher Resistance).
• Wide pipe ($A$) = More space, making it easier for water to flow (Lower Resistance).
  • Resistance is directly proportional to length ($R \propto l$).
  • Resistance is inversely proportional to area ($R \propto 1/A$).
R ∝ lA ➔ R = ρ lA
Teacher's Desk In this formula, $\rho$ (rho) is a constant called Resistivity. Note that $R$ also depends on the nature of material and temperature.

Unit: Ohm (Ω)
🧪 2. Resistivity / Specific Resistance (ρ)

Unlike resistance, resistivity is an Intrinsic Property of matter. It does not depend on the shape or size of the conductor, only on its Nature and Temperature.

Definition: "It is the resistance of a conductor that is 1 meter long and has a cross-sectional area of 1 square meter."
ρ = R × Al
Identity of Matter 1 gram of copper and 1 ton of copper have different Resistance ($R$), but they have the same **Resistivity ($\rho$)** because the material's nature remains unchanged!

Unit: Ohm-meter (Ω·m)
💡 3. Conductance (G or C)

Conductance is simply the reciprocal of resistance. It represents the ease with which current flows through a conductor or solution.

G = 1R
Unit: ohm⁻¹ , mho, or Siemens (S).
In modern electrolytic chemistry, S is the standard unit.
🔍 4. Conductivity / Specific Conductance (κ)

The reciprocal of resistivity ($\rho$) is called Conductivity (Kappa).
In physical terms: It is the conductance of exactly 1 cubic centimeter ($1 cm^{3}$) of an electrolytic solution.

κ = 1ρ = G × lA
Core Insight
The ratio $\frac{l}{A}$ is known as the Cell Constant (G*).

Formula: Conductivity = Conductance × Cell Constant

Unit: S·m⁻¹ or S·cm⁻¹
📑 Classification according to Conductivity

Based on conductivity, substances are of three types -

1) Conductors:
Substances that allow electricity to flow through them easily are called conductors.
2) Insulators:
Substances that do not allow electric current to flow are called insulators.
Example: Rubber, Wood, Glass etc.
3) Semiconductors:
Substances whose conductivity lies between conductors and insulators are called semiconductors.
Ex: Group-13, Group-14, Group-15 elements
1. Metallic Conductors: Flow of electrons through a metal lattice (e.g., Copper wire). No mass transfer occurs.

2. Electrolytic Conductors: Flow of Ions through a liquid or solution. Involves the actual movement of particles.

Types of Electrolytes

Strong Electrolytes

Substances that dissociate completely into ions (~100%).

Ex: NaCl, HCl, NaOH, KNO₃

Weak Electrolytes

Substances that partially dissociate into ions.

Ex: CH₃COOH, NH₄OH, H₂CO₃
🏛️ Factors Affecting Conductivity

1) Nature of Electrolyte -

  • If the electrolyte is **Strong**, it dissociates completely in solution. Hence, as the number of ions increases, conductivity also increases.
  • If the electrolyte is **Weak**, it does not dissociate completely in solution, leading to fewer ions and lower conductivity.

2) Nature of Solvent -

  • In a **Polar solvent**, electrolytes dissociate easily, leading to an increase in conductivity values.
  • In a **Non-polar solvent**, electrolytes do not dissociate easily, resulting in a decrease in conductivity.

3) Dilution -

➔ As the dilution of a solution increases, the number of ions per unit volume decreases. Therefore, conductivity (κ) decreases with an increase in dilution.

4) Temperature -

➔ As temperature increases, the kinetic energy of the ions in the solution increases. Therefore, the value of conductivity also increases.
