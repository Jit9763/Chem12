Commercial Cells & Corrosion

Part 10 (English Portal)

➔ Commercial Cells / Batteries
"When two or more Galvanic cells are connected in series, the arrangement is called a Battery."
"Thus, a battery is an electrochemical device used as an energy source."
"Commercial cells or batteries are actually Galvanic Cells in which chemical energy is converted directly into electrical energy."

📝 Requirements of a Good Battery:

  1. It should be Lightweight and Compact.
  2. The Voltage should not vary significantly during usage.
  3. It should be Durable and long-lasting.

1. Primary Cells

These cells cannot be recharged. The reaction happens only once and they become inactive after some time.
Ex: Dry Cell, Mercury Cell.

2. Secondary Cells

These cells can be recharged and used repeatedly.
Ex: Lead Storage Battery, Ni-Cd Cell.

➔ 1. Dry Cell (Leclanche Cell)

➔ Also known as a 'Leclanche cell'. It is commonly used in transistors, clocks, etc.

🔋 Labeled Diagram of Dry Cell

🏗️ Construction & Working:

  • In a dry cell, the Zinc (Zn) metal container acts as the anode, and the Graphite rod acts as the cathode.
  • A Brass cap is fixed atop the graphite rod. Around this cap are microscopic pores through which gases produced during the chemical reaction can escape.
  • The graphite rod is surrounded by MnO₂ and C powder, and the space is filled with a paste of NH₄Cl and ZnCl₂ (Electrolyte).

🧪 Cell Reactions:

At Anode: Zn(s) ➔ Zn²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻
At Cathode: MnO₂ + NH₄⁺ + e⁻ ➔ MnO(OH) + NH₃

Manganese (Mn) is reduced from +4 to +3 state here. Note that the liberated Ammonia (NH₃) gas reacts with Zn²⁺ to form the [Zn(NH₃)₄]²⁺ complex, preventing pressure buildup. Potential is between 1.25V and 1.5V.

➔ 2. Mercury Cell (Button Cell)

🔋 Labeled Diagram of Mercury Cell

  • In Mercury cells, Zinc-Amalgam acts as the anode and a paste of C + HgO acts as the cathode.
  • KOH, NaOH and ZnO paste acts as the electrolyte.
  • The cathode and anode are kept separate by a Separator.
  • The cell potential is 1.35 V.

🧪 Mechanism (Cell Reactions):

At Anode: Zn + 2OH⁻ ➔ ZnO + H₂O + 2e⁻
At Cathode: HgO + H₂O + 2e⁻ ➔ Hg + 2OH⁻
Net Cell Rexn: Zn + HgO ➔ ZnO + Hg

🛠️ Usage: Clocks, Watches, Hearing aids.

Technical Logic: The potential remains stable throughout its life because no ions are overall consumed or produced whose concentration can change in the net cell reaction.

➔ Secondary Cell: Lead Storage Battery
"The Lead Storage Battery is a accumulator cell made of Lead and Lead Oxide. The grids filled with Lead (Pb) serve as the Anode, while those filled with Lead Dioxide (PbO₂) serve as the Cathode."

➔ It contains 38% H₂SO₄ solution as the electrolyte (Density = 1.30 g/cm³).

➔ During discharge, the concentration of ions starts to decrease, while during recharging, it gradually increases.

➔ 3. Nickel-Cadmium Cell (Ni-Cd)
"In this cell, Cadmium (Cd) acts as the Anode and Nickel (Ni) acts as the Cathode. A Separator Plate is present between the Negative and Positive plates."

➔ Electrolyte: Aqueous solution of NaOH and KOH is used.

➔ The potential of this cell is 1.4 V.

➔ It has a longer life than lead storage batteries but is more expensive to manufacture.

🔋 Ni-Cd Labeled Diagram (Jelly-Roll Structure)

Electrolyte: NaOH + KOH

🧪 Mechanism:

  • Oxidation occurs at Anode; Reduction at Cathode.
  • Usage: Calculators, Electronic Watches.

🔬 Cell Reactions:

At Anode: Cd(s) + 2OH⁻ ➔ Cd(OH)₂(s) + 2e⁻
At Cathode: NiO₂ + 2H₂O + 2e⁻ ➔ Ni(OH)₂ + 2OH⁻
Net Rxn: Cd + NiO₂ + 2H₂O ➔ Cd(OH)₂ + Ni(OH)₂
➔ Fuel Cells (Clean Energy)
"Cells that convert the combustion energy of fuels (like H₂, CH₄, CH₃OH) directly into electrical energy."

➔ H₂-O₂ Fuel Cell: Electrodes are made of porous carbon containing Pt/Pd catalyst powder.

➔ Used in the Apollo Space Program. Electrolyte is aqueous KOH or NaOH.

➔ Corrosion
"The formation of a layer of oxides or other salts (sulfides, sulfates, and carbonates) on the surface of metals in the presence of water/moisture and air is called Corrosion."

➔ Examples:

(i) Rusting of Iron.

(ii) Blackening (Tarnishing) of silver surface.

(iii) Green spots on Copper and Brass utensils.

🔧 Mechanism of Rusting (Iron)

➔ Pure Iron comes in contact with moisture and atmospheric oxygen and starts acting as the Anode.

➔ Impurities present on the iron surface act as the Cathode, thus forming an Electrochemical Cell.

➔ Moisture reacts with atmospheric CO₂ to form Carbonic Acid (H₂CO₃), which provides H⁺ ions.

🔬 Rusting Chemical Process (Droplet Mechanism)

🧪 Cell Equations:

At Anode: 2Fe ➔ 2Fe²⁺ + 4e⁻
At Cathode: O₂ + 4H⁺ + 4e⁻ ➔ 2H₂O
Net Rexn: 2Fe + O₂ + 4H⁺ ➔ 2Fe²⁺ + 2H₂O

➔ Fe²⁺ ions react with atmospheric oxygen and convert into Fe₂O₃.
➔ This Fe₂O₃ becomes hydrated to form Rust:

Fe₂O₃ + xH₂O ➔ Fe₂O₃ ⋅ xH₂O (Rust)

🛡️ Prevention of Corrosion

i) Corrosion can be prevented by using grease, paint, varnish, etc.

ii) By coating a layer of a more reactive metal on iron. E.g., Coating iron with Zn is called Galvanization.

iii) This phenomenon is also known as Electroplating or Cathodic Protection. It is an electrochemical method.
